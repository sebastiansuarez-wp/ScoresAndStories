# The Wagner Prep Opportunity Research Playbook
## Scores and Stories: A Recurring Research System for Timely Student Opportunities

**Document status:** Internal. Living document. Built June 2026.
**Owner:** Sebastian, with review from Mike, Megan, Marley.
**Cadence:** The research protocol in Part 7 runs at minimum every 3 days.
**What this is:** The complete thinking behind, and the operating manual for, Wagner Prep's recurring opportunity research engine. It defines who we research for, what counts as a real opportunity, where to look, how to rank what we find, and the exact prompts that keep the feed running on schedule with real, dated, verifiable opportunities.

# Part 1: Why This Document Exists

## 1.1 The problem we are solving

Wagner Prep's value to families is not access to secret information. Everything we surface is technically public. Our value is curation, timing, and translation. A teen night at the Brooklyn Museum is just an event listing until a counselor connects it to a specific tenth grader's emerging interest in curation and tells the family two weeks before it happens instead of two days after.

The problem is that timely curation does not scale by memory. There are hundreds of institutions in the New York area running thousands of programs per year, with application windows that open and close on rhythms nobody fully tracks. A counselor working a full caseload cannot also be a full time events researcher. So opportunities get surfaced reactively, when a parent forwards a flyer, or when a student happens to mention something. That is backwards. We want to be the ones forwarding the flyer.

The second problem is content. Wagner Prep's blog and webinar pipeline needs a steady supply of timely, locally grounded material. Generic admissions content ("How to write a great essay") is a crowded field where we compete with every national firm and every free YouTube channel. Hyper local, time sensitive content ("Five free things happening in NYC this month that belong on a future Common App") is a field where almost nobody competes, because almost nobody does the research consistently. Consistency is the moat.

This document solves both problems with one system. Every research cycle produces two outputs from the same work: a ranked internal list of opportunities to push to specific students, and a shortlist of blog and social angles for Megan's content pipeline.

## 1.2 What this document is not

This is not a static list of opportunities. Lists rot. Application windows close, programs get discontinued, museums restructure their teen offerings. Any specific program named in this document is named as a research target and a pattern example, not as a confirmed current offering. Every cycle re-verifies against live sources.

This is also not a replacement for counselor judgment. The ranking rubric in Part 6 produces a score, and the score is a sorting tool, not a decision. A counselor who knows that Gael responds to hands on workshops and shuts down in lecture formats will override the rubric, and should.

## 1.3 How to use this document

* **If you are running a research cycle:** go straight to Part 7 for the protocol and Part 8 for the master prompt. Everything before that is the reasoning the prompt encodes.
* **If you are reviewing feed output:** use the rubric in Part 6 and the verification checklist in Part 13.
* **If you are writing a blog post from the feed:** use the editorial archetypes in Part 11.
* **If you are onboarding someone new to the system:** read Parts 2 through 5 in order. That is the worldview. The rest is machinery.

# Part 2: The Scores and Stories Framework as a Research Lens

## 2.1 The framework in one paragraph

Every admissions outcome is built from two kinds of evidence. **Scores** are the quantitative and credentialed signals: GPA, course rigor, standardized testing, awards with selective admission rates, research output, measurable skill certifications. **Stories** are the narrative and experiential signals: the lived experiences, sustained interests, communities, and projects that give an application a voice, a theme, and a reason to be remembered. Selective admissions offices read both. A student strong on Scores but empty on Stories reads as a transcript. A student rich in Stories but thin on Scores reads as untested. Wagner Prep's positioning is that we build both deliberately, and that the building happens years before the application is written.

## 2.2 Why opportunities are the connective tissue

Here is the insight that makes opportunity research central rather than peripheral to the framework. Scores and Stories are usually treated as separate workstreams. Test prep over here, "find your passion" over there. But the local opportunity ecosystem is where the two converge, because a single well chosen opportunity often feeds both.

Consider a free genomics lecture at a science academy. A ninth grader who attends has a Story seed (the moment she realized lab science was about argument, not memorization). If the lecture leads her to a summer research program, that becomes a Scores asset (research output, a recommendation from a scientist, possibly a competition entry). One free Tuesday evening, two ledgers credited.

This is why the feed does not just collect events. It tags every opportunity by which ledger it feeds:

* **Stories:** Exposure, experience, community, narrative material. Low barrier, often free, often one time. The raw material of essays and interviews.
* **Scores:** Credential, output, measurable skill, selectivity. Usually application based, sustained, sometimes costly. The raw material of the activities list and honors section.
* **Both:** The highest value category. Opportunities that start as exposure and contain a pathway to credential, or credentials that generate genuine narrative.

## 2.3 The ladder logic

The framework implies a progression we should actively look for in the ecosystem. Call it the opportunity ladder:

1. **Rung 1, Exposure (Stories):** Free, drop in, no application. Museum events, public lectures, festivals, open studios. The job of Rung 1 is to generate curiosity and a first data point. "I went, and I felt something."
2. **Rung 2, Engagement (Stories, edging toward Both):** Recurring but low commitment. Teen councils, volunteer shifts, workshop series, library programs. The job of Rung 2 is consistency and community. "I kept going back."
3. **Rung 3, Production (Both):** The student makes something or holds responsibility. Internships, juried showcases, hackathon teams, published work, leadership in a recurring program. "I built something and someone outside my family saw it."
4. **Rung 4, Credential (Scores):** Selective, evaluated, externally validated. Competitive research programs, national competitions, paid internships with selective admission, awards. "An institution chose me."

The research engine should surface opportunities at every rung, and the ranking system in Part 6 explicitly rewards opportunities that connect rungs (an exposure event run by an institution that also offers a selective program, for example, because attending the former is the smartest possible preparation for applying to the latter).

This ladder is also the spine of our content positioning. Most admissions content talks only about Rung 4 and induces panic. Wagner Prep content that walks families up the ladder, starting from a free Saturday event, is calmer, more actionable, and more honest about how strong applications are actually built. That calm is the brand.

## 2.4 Anti-patterns the framework warns against

The lens also tells us what NOT to surface, which matters as much as what we do surface:

* **Pay to play exposure dressed as credential.** Expensive "leadership summits" and "youth honor societies" that admit anyone who pays. These feed neither ledger honestly. Admissions readers discount them. We flag these in the feed as "vetting risk" when they appear, because parents WILL ask about them, and being the firm that says "skip it, here is a free alternative that reads better" is a trust builder. The exception is a pay to play program with a genuine scholarship track or unusually strong mentorship, which we evaluate case by case.
* **Resume line items with no narrative.** One off certificates, single day "conferences" with attendance certificates. A Saturday certificate is not a Story and barely a Score.
* **Mismatch by grade band.** A selective research program is a poor recommendation for a ninth grader who has never set foot in the field. Rung skipping produces rejection and discouragement. The feed tags grade fit for this reason.
* **Geographic fantasy.** An amazing program in Boston is not amazing for a Queens family without the means or appetite for travel. Hence the geographic rings in Part 3.5 and the priority weighting they carry.

# Part 3: The Audience, Deeply

This is the longest analytical section because everything downstream depends on it. The recurring prompt is only as good as its model of who the research serves.

## 3.1 Two audiences in one

Every opportunity we surface serves two distinct readers, and the feed must satisfy both:

**The internal reader** is a Wagner Prep counselor with a live caseload. They scan the feed asking one question: "Who on my list is this for?" They need precision. Specific dates, specific costs, specific application requirements, a one line student fit note. They will tolerate density. They are allergic to vagueness, because vagueness means they have to do the verification work themselves before they can forward anything to a family.

**The external reader** is the family, reached through blog posts, the newsletter, webinars, and direct counselor recommendations built from the feed. They need translation. Not "applications open for the museum's teen apprenticeship," but "if your tenth grader liked the Egyptian wing more than the gift shop, here is the program that turns that into something, and the deadline is in three weeks." The external reader is, in most cases, the parent first and the student second, and that asymmetry shapes everything in Section 3.3.

## 3.2 The student archetypes

Wagner Prep's internal vocabulary describes students along an arc from Explore to Evaluate to Execute, and we layer the T-shaped model on top: depth in one or two areas, breadth across several. The opportunity feed serves different archetypes differently. These archetypes are composites for research targeting purposes, not labels we put on real students.

**The Undecided Explorer (typically 9th, early 10th grade).**
Has interests but no theme. Tries things and drops them. The parent is anxious about "falling behind" and the student is anxious about being asked what they want to do with their life. What this archetype needs from the feed: Rung 1 and Rung 2 opportunities across multiple fields, all free or near free, low commitment, high novelty. The win condition is a spark, not a credential. The feed should never make an Explorer family feel like they are already late. Content tone for this archetype: invitational, curiosity forward, explicitly anti panic.

**The Single Track Specialist (any grade, concentrated in 10th and 11th).**
Already deep in one thing: a coder, a dancer, a debater, a sailor. The risk profile is inverted. They do not need exposure, they need elevation (the next rung in their field) and broadening (one adjacent field that keeps the T-shape from becoming an I-shape). What this archetype needs from the feed: Rung 3 and Rung 4 opportunities in their depth field, plus deliberately chosen Rung 1 events in adjacent fields. A film kid gets the selective animation internship AND the free architecture lecture, because the second one is where the surprising essay comes from.

**The Builder (often STEM or entrepreneurial, often male skewing in our caseload but not exclusively).**
Learns by making. Bored by lectures, lit up by hackathons, maker spaces, regattas, robotics. School based metrics often undersell them. What this archetype needs from the feed: anything with output. Competitions, build events, project based volunteering, internships with deliverables. The Story for a Builder is always the artifact, so the feed should flag opportunities where something tangible gets made and can later be shown.

**The Performer and Portfolio Artist (film, fine art, music, theater, dance).**
Their Scores ledger partially IS their Stories ledger, because portfolios are evaluated artifacts. NYC is the single richest environment in the country for this archetype, which makes the feed disproportionately valuable for them. What they need: juried showcases, open submission opportunities, free masterclasses with working artists, museum teen programs at institutions whose names carry weight, screening and exhibition opportunities. Deadlines matter enormously here because portfolio seasons are rigid.

**The Service Hearted Connector (civic, health, community oriented).**
Motivated by people and impact. At risk of accumulating scattered volunteer hours that read as generic. What this archetype needs from the feed: sustained service roles with growth tracks (Rung 2 into Rung 3), civic fellowships, hospital and public health youth programs, advocacy opportunities tied to a consistent theme. The counseling move is consolidation: fewer organizations, deeper roles. The feed supports that by flagging which service opportunities offer recurring involvement versus one off hours.

**The Quiet Academic (humanities heavy, often introverted, strong transcript).**
Strong Scores, thin Stories, and often resistant to manufactured extracurricular life. The trap is forcing them into activities that read as fake because they are fake. What this archetype needs from the feed: opportunities where the intellectual life IS the activity. Author talks, university lectures open to the public, essay and writing competitions, archival and library teen programs, reading groups, research mentorship. Their Story is the life of the mind made visible, and NYC's lecture ecosystem is built for exactly this.

**The Late Starter (11th grade, sometimes early 12th, new to the process).**
Time compressed. Cannot ladder slowly. What they need from the feed: Both tagged opportunities with near term deadlines and fast cycles, plus realistic framing. The feed's timeliness is most valuable here, because for a Late Starter, finding out about a deadline two weeks early versus two days late is the entire ballgame.

**The International or Newly Arrived Student.**
Wagner Prep's international school relationships mean we serve students new to the US system or applying from abroad, plus NYC students at international curricula schools. They may not know what is normal, may face eligibility restrictions (some programs require US citizenship or specific visa status, some city programs require NYC residency or DOE enrollment), and may have language considerations. What this archetype needs: opportunities with clear eligibility statements, virtual options weighted slightly higher than for other archetypes, and explicit flags when citizenship, residency, or school enrollment requirements apply. The feed must check eligibility language because nothing erodes family trust faster than recommending a program a student cannot legally join.

## 3.3 The parent as reader

In the vast majority of our engagements, the parent is the buyer, the forwarder, and the scheduler. The student is the doer. The feed's external facing language must therefore work on the parent without alienating the student. Some observed truths about our parent audience, stated plainly:

* **They are time poor and anxiety rich.** The dominant emotional state of an NYC admissions parent is low grade dread that they are missing something everyone else knows. Our content's job is to convert that dread into a small, concrete, dated action. "This Saturday, free, no registration, just go" is therapeutic in a way no reassuring essay about holistic admissions will ever be.
* **They respond to institutional names.** A workshop at a name brand museum gets forwarded; an equally good workshop at an unknown community space does not, unless we do the work of explaining why it is good. This is a bias we both serve and gently correct. Serve it by leading with recognizable institutions. Correct it by making "the hidden gem" a recurring content format with our credibility behind it.
* **They conflate cost with value.** The 5,000 dollar summer program feels more legitimate than the free city program, when admissions readers often weight them in exactly the opposite direction (the free program was selective, the paid one was not). This misconception is one of our best content angles, because correcting it saves families money, which builds the kind of trust that money cannot buy. Every cycle, the feed should be able to support a "free beats paid" comparison if one exists that period.
* **Logistics decide everything.** A Manhattan parent will not send a fifteen year old to an evening event in a borough they cannot picture. Commute time, transit access, end time, and whether a parent can plausibly accompany are real selection criteria. The feed records location at the neighborhood level and notes end times for evening events when available, because that detail is the difference between forwarded and ignored.
* **They forward things as care.** A parent sending an opportunity to their kid (and to other parents) is performing attentiveness and love. Our content should be built to be forwarded: self contained, dated, specific, and generous. Every blog post from this feed is also a referral mechanism, because the parent who forwards our roundup is endorsing us to their network.

## 3.4 Grade band needs, mapped to the calendar

The same opportunity means different things in different grades, and the feed weights accordingly.

**9th grade: the year of sampling.** Everything is Rung 1 and 2. The metric of a good ninth grade year is the number of genuine data points generated about what the student likes, not the number of commitments accumulated. Feed priority: breadth, freeness, low stakes. Key calendar moments: fall open house season at cultural institutions, winter teen program application windows for spring cohorts, spring previews of summer opportunities.

**10th grade: the year of choosing.** Sampling narrows into two or three threads. Rung 2 deepens, Rung 3 begins. This is the most important year for the opportunity ladder, because choices made here determine what 11th grade applications (to programs, not colleges) are credible. Feed priority: recurring programs, teen councils, first competitions, first selective applications with forgiving admission rates. Key calendar moments: December through February, when most selective summer program applications for the following summer are due, lands in the middle of 10th grade and is the highest leverage deadline window in the entire system.

**11th grade: the year of producing.** Rung 3 and 4. Research programs, internships, leadership, awards. Everything done this year is directly visible to colleges. Feed priority: selective opportunities, output opportunities, anything that generates a recommender relationship with an adult who is not a school teacher. Key calendar moments: the same December through February window for the summer before senior year (the single most consequential summer), plus spring competition deadlines, plus fall research program cycles.

**12th grade: the year of harvesting, with one twist.** Mostly the student converts existing material into applications. But the feed still matters twice over. First semester: opportunities that can still appear in applications or interviews ("last month I attended..."  is legitimate interview material and signals continued engagement). Second semester: maintaining momentum and depth for the transition, plus opportunities that fight senioritis. Feed priority: low commitment Rung 1 events that keep the intellectual life visibly alive, plus any genuinely late breaking Rung 4 items (some scholarships and competitions run deep into senior year).

## 3.5 Geography: the four rings

The feed prioritizes geography in concentric rings, and the master prompt allocates research attention accordingly.

**Ring 1: The five boroughs (roughly 60 percent of research attention).**
Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, Bronx, Staten Island. This is home turf and the heart of the brand. Hyper local is the differentiator: nobody else is telling Bay Ridge families about the thing happening in their own borough this weekend. Within Ring 1, attend to borough equity. The default failure mode of NYC events research is that everything is in Manhattan below 96th Street plus brownstone Brooklyn. Queens, the Bronx, and Staten Island have real institutions (a world class film museum in Astoria, a botanical garden and zoo ecosystem in the Bronx, cultural institutions on Staten Island) and our outer borough families notice when they only ever see Manhattan listings. Each research cycle should consciously check at least one outer borough source.

**Ring 2: The commutable belt (roughly 25 percent of attention).**
Long Island (Nassau primarily), Westchester and the lower Hudson Valley, North and Central New Jersey, Southwestern Connecticut. Rule of thumb: reachable within about 90 minutes by regional rail (LIRR, Metro North, NJ Transit) from Midtown. This ring matters for three reasons. First, some of our families live here. Second, some categories (corporate internship programs in Stamford or Jersey City, lab programs at Long Island research institutions, university programs in Westchester and New Jersey) are structurally located here. Third, weekend trips into Ring 2 are plausible for Ring 1 families when the opportunity justifies it, and saying so explicitly ("worth the LIRR ride") is a useful editorial voice.

**Ring 3: The Northeast corridor (roughly 10 percent of attention).**
Boston, New Haven, Philadelphia, Princeton, Washington DC at the far edge. Only opportunities that clear a higher bar: selective, residential or single event, prestigious enough to justify travel, or uniquely unduplicated in Rings 1 and 2. A selective summer residential program at a Boston area university belongs in the feed. A public lecture in Philadelphia does not, because an equivalent exists in Manhattan this same week. Ring 3 items are almost always Rung 3 or 4.

**Ring 4: Online (roughly 5 percent of attention, explicitly lower priority).**
Virtual programs, remote competitions, online research mentorship, MOOCs with verified credentials, virtual lecture series. Lower priority for two reasons. First, online opportunities are not time or place scarce, so they do not benefit from our timeliness advantage and do not differentiate the blog. Second, they are weaker Stories material (the essay about the Zoom program is structurally disadvantaged against the essay about the place). But Ring 4 earns its 5 percent through specific cases: international and mobility limited students, niche fields with no local presence, genuinely selective national virtual programs, and deep winter weeks when the local calendar thins. Ring 4 items should be selective or output producing to make the feed; virtual exposure events generally should not.

## 3.6 Socioeconomic range and the free first principle

Wagner Prep's client base skews toward families who can afford private counseling, but the feed operates free first anyway, for four reasons that are worth stating because they drive ranking weights:

1. **Admissions value:** free public programs are often more selective and better regarded than paid ones, as covered in 3.3.
2. **Blog reach:** free content is forwardable to everyone, which grows the audience beyond current clients. A roundup of 5,000 dollar programs serves only clients; a roundup of free programs serves the whole school community a client family belongs to, with our name on it.
3. **Trust positioning:** the firm that consistently surfaces free opportunities is visibly not upselling. That credibility is the precondition for being believed when we do recommend something paid.
4. **Sibling and scholarship dynamics:** even affluent families have constraints, multiple children, and a strong appetite for value. Nobody has ever resented being told about something excellent and free.

Paid opportunities still appear in the feed when they clear the value bar (genuine selectivity, scholarship availability, unique access), and the feed always records cost honestly, including hidden costs like materials fees and travel.

# Part 4: The Field Taxonomy

The feed scans six fields. Each field below includes its sub interests, its archetype affinities, its characteristic opportunity types at each ladder rung, and field specific research notes. The taxonomy exists so that research cycles can rotate focus systematically (Part 7) instead of defaulting to whatever is easiest to find, which in NYC is always arts events in Manhattan.

## 4.1 Arts and Film

**Sub interests:** narrative film, documentary, animation and stop motion, photography, fine art and curation, fashion and design, music performance and production, theater and musical theater, dance, creative writing, architecture.

**Archetype affinity:** Performer and Portfolio Artist primarily; Explorer for Rung 1; Builder for animation, design, and architecture.

**Ladder by rung:**
* Rung 1: museum free nights and teen nights, gallery openings, free screenings and Q&As, open rehearsals, public design talks, free festival days.
* Rung 2: museum teen councils and apprentice programs, free or low cost workshop series at film and media organizations, library media labs, community theater involvement, writing workshops.
* Rung 3: juried teen exhibitions and film festivals with open submission, zine and publication opportunities, teen curatorial programs, production internships, portfolio development programs at art institutions.
* Rung 4: nationally recognized art and writing awards with regional NYC affiliates, selective pre college portfolio programs, competitive film fellowships for teens, conservatory youth programs with auditions.

**Research notes:** NYC is the deepest market in the country for this field, so the constraint is selection, not supply. Watch the major museums' education calendars (the encyclopedic art museums, the modern art museums, the film museum in Astoria, the design museums), the festival calendar (film festivals across boroughs cluster in spring and fall), and the awards calendar (the major national teen art and writing award has regional deadlines typically in December, verify each cycle). Portfolio season for visual arts runs roughly September through January, which means feed items about portfolio building programs are most actionable May through September, before the crunch.

## 4.2 STEM and Tech

**Sub interests:** computer science and software, robotics and engineering, mathematics, physics and astronomy, biology and genomics, chemistry, environmental science, data science and AI, maker culture and fabrication, aviation and maritime tech.

**Archetype affinity:** Builder primarily; Quiet Academic for math and research strands; Single Track Specialist common here.

**Ladder by rung:**
* Rung 1: science museum public programs and astronomy nights, university public lectures, tech meetups with all ages access, maker faire style events, aquarium and botanical science events.
* Rung 2: museum science clubs and after school programs, library maker spaces and coding clubs, citizen science projects with NYC chapters, math circles, astronomy clubs.
* Rung 3: hackathons open to high schoolers, robotics teams and competitions, science fair pipelines, environmental monitoring volunteer programs, teen tech internships at nonprofits.
* Rung 4: selective research mentorship programs (the city's specialized science research ecosystem, university lab placements, nationally competitive science fairs and olympiads, selective summer STEM institutes).

**Research notes:** The Scores ledger dominates this field at upper rungs, so deadline precision matters most here. The major selective research programs and summer institutes overwhelmingly take applications December through February. Science fair season runs January through May with school level entry points in fall. Hackathons are year round but cluster in academic semesters. University public lecture series follow the academic calendar (September to May), and several NYC universities and academies run public science events that are free, prestigious feeling, and chronically underattended by exactly the families who would benefit, which makes them superb feed material.

## 4.3 Humanities and History

**Sub interests:** history and archives, literature and book culture, philosophy, classics, languages, journalism, law and constitutional studies, religion and ethics, museums and material culture.

**Archetype affinity:** Quiet Academic primarily; Service Hearted Connector for journalism and law strands.

**Ladder by rung:**
* Rung 1: author talks and readings (the city's bookstores, libraries, and cultural centers run these nightly), history museum programs, archive open houses, courtroom observation (public trials are public), heritage festivals.
* Rung 2: library teen programs and book clubs, historical society youth programs, school newspaper plus city journalism workshops, debate leagues, language and culture institutes' youth offerings.
* Rung 3: essay competitions with real selectivity, teen archival and oral history projects, student journalism with publication, mock trial and moot court, museum education volunteering.
* Rung 4: prestigious national essay and history competitions, selective humanities summer programs, journalism fellowships for teens, classics and language olympiads.

**Research notes:** This field is the feed's secret weapon for the Quiet Academic archetype because NYC's lecture and reading ecosystem is enormous, free, and almost entirely unmarketed to high schoolers. Nobody tells a tenth grader that a major public intellectual is speaking for free at a library branch on Thursday. We can be the ones who do. The cultural center on the Upper East Side, the main library's event calendar, the historical societies in multiple boroughs, and the university public humanities programs are standing sources. Essay competition deadlines scatter across the year, which makes them reliable feed filler in thin weeks.

## 4.4 Civic and Social Impact

**Sub interests:** local government and policy, community organizing, social justice and advocacy, urban planning and housing, immigration, public interest law, environmental justice, philanthropy and nonprofit work.

**Archetype affinity:** Service Hearted Connector primarily; ties strongly to the city's official youth civic infrastructure.

**Ladder by rung:**
* Rung 1: community board meetings (open to the public, and a community board meeting is a genuinely underrated Story setting), town halls, public hearings, civic tech meetups, participatory budgeting events.
* Rung 2: sustained volunteering with growth tracks, youth advisory boards, borough president youth programs, mutual aid involvement, voter registration drives.
* Rung 3: city youth leadership councils, teen advocacy fellowships, participatory budgeting youth committees, organizing internships at nonprofits.
* Rung 4: the city's official youth employment and civic programs with selective tracks, prestigious civic fellowships, national civic competitions, elected youth positions.

**Research notes:** The city government itself is a major program operator in this field (youth employment programs, youth leadership councils, parks and library systems), and government program calendars are rigid and predictable, which suits a recurring feed. The flagship city summer employment program for youth typically opens applications in winter for summer placement; verify the current year's window each cycle because it moves. Civic opportunities are also the most residency restricted category (many city programs require NYC residency and sometimes public school enrollment), so eligibility flags matter most here.

## 4.5 Business and Entrepreneurship

**Sub interests:** entrepreneurship and startups, finance and investing, marketing, economics, real estate, hospitality, sports business, fashion business.

**Archetype affinity:** Builder and Single Track Specialist; also the field where parent enthusiasm most outruns student interest, which counselors should watch for.

**Ladder by rung:**
* Rung 1: startup pitch events open to the public, business school public lectures, fintech and industry conference free tracks, entrepreneurship fair days.
* Rung 2: investment clubs (school based plus city youth versions), business plan workshop series, junior achievement style programs, library business center youth offerings.
* Rung 3: teen business plan competitions, startup internships, student run ventures with real revenue, sales and marketing roles in family or community businesses formalized into real responsibility.
* Rung 4: selective business and finance summer programs at universities, national business competitions, banks' and firms' selective high school programs (several major financial institutions run them in NYC, verify current cycles).

**Research notes:** This field has the worst pay to play problem of the six. A large share of teen "business programs" are expensive credential theater. The vetting bar is highest here, and the feed should apply the anti-pattern filter from 2.4 aggressively. The genuinely valuable items are the free competitions, the selective bank and firm programs, and real internships, and they are scarce enough that surfacing them reliably is high value.

## 4.6 Health and Science of Care

**Sub interests:** medicine and pre health, nursing, public health, psychology and neuroscience, child life and human development, sports medicine and kinesiology, nutrition, veterinary and animal science, EMS and emergency response.

**Archetype affinity:** Service Hearted Connector primarily; Quiet Academic for the research strands; directly relevant to multiple current caseload students with nursing and child life interests.

**Ladder by rung:**
* Rung 1: hospital community education events, public health lectures, brain awareness events, blood drives (as volunteer not just donor), health fairs.
* Rung 2: hospital junior volunteer programs (most major NYC systems run them with seasonal application windows), certified first aid and CPR training, peer health educator programs, animal shelter volunteering.
* Rung 3: clinical shadowing programs where available to minors, EMS youth corps and explorer programs, health research volunteer roles, hospital teen advisory councils.
* Rung 4: selective medical and public health summer pipeline programs (several NYC medical schools and hospital systems run them, often targeting underrepresented students, eligibility checks essential), national health research competitions, certified EMT training for eligible ages.

**Research notes:** Hospital volunteer applications have long lead times (application, health clearance, training) of often two to three months before a student starts, so the feed should surface these windows early. Age minimums vary by institution (commonly 14 to 16) and must be recorded. The selective pipeline programs at medical schools are among the strongest Both opportunities in the entire ecosystem for the right student, and their eligibility criteria (residency, school type, demographic focus) must be reported precisely and respectfully.

## 4.7 Cross field events

Some of the best material refuses the taxonomy: large festivals, citywide open house weekends (the city has a famous one in fall where normally closed buildings open free to the public), idea festivals, TED format events, world's fair style expos, university open campus days. These get tagged with a primary field plus a cross field note, and they rank well because they serve the Explorer archetype better than anything else in the system.

# Part 5: The Source Map

This is the standing inventory of where the research looks. Sources are organized by ring and type. Institution categories are stable; specific program names and URLs change, which is why the protocol verifies everything live each cycle. The master prompt references this map by category rather than hardcoding URLs.

## 5.1 Ring 1 standing sources

**The encyclopedic and major museums (Manhattan):** the major art museums on and off the park, the natural history museum, the modern art institutions, the design museums, the city history museum. All run education calendars with teen specific programming, free nights, and seasonal application windows for councils and apprenticeships. Check education and teen pages specifically, not just main event calendars, because teen programs are often buried.

**The outer borough anchors:** the Brooklyn art museum and botanic garden, the Brooklyn academy of arts programming, the film museum in Astoria, the Queens art museum and hall of science, the Bronx zoo and botanical garden education programs, the Bronx art museum, Staten Island's cultural institutions and museum campus. At least one outer borough anchor gets checked every cycle by protocol rule.

**The library systems (three of them):** the city's three library systems (Manhattan plus the Bronx and Staten Island in one, Brooklyn in another, Queens in the third) are collectively the largest free youth program operator in the region: teen centers, maker labs, college prep events, author talks, volunteer opportunities. Their event calendars are searchable and dense. The feed should never run a cycle without a library system check, because library programs are free by definition and hyper local by structure.

**The science and research institutions:** the science academy in Manhattan that runs public programs and afterschool offerings, the hall of science in Queens, university public lecture calendars (multiple universities uptown, downtown, and in Brooklyn), the genome center and similar institutes' public education arms, the aquarium and wildlife conservation education programs.

**The civic infrastructure:** city youth services pages, the parks department events and volunteer calendars, borough presidents' offices, community board calendars, the city's official events portal, participatory budgeting cycles, the major youth employment program's announcements.

**The performance and film institutions:** the performing arts complex uptown and its free programming, the downtown arts spaces, the film societies and their festivals, borough arts councils which aggregate local events and grants.

**The universities as public venues:** nearly every NYC university runs free public events (lectures, panels, screenings, open houses). University event calendars are high yield because they signal intellectual seriousness to families and are reliably free. Pre college program announcements also live here, with the caveat that university pre college offerings span the full quality and cost spectrum and need the 2.4 anti-pattern filter.

**TED format and ideas events:** the main TED organization is headquartered in NYC; independently organized TEDx events occur across the boroughs and Ring 2 on rolling schedules, often with free or cheap registration and sometimes with youth speaker opportunities, which are a Story goldmine. Idea festivals and talk series at the major cultural centers belong in this bucket.

## 5.2 Ring 2 standing sources

**Long Island:** the major lab in Cold Spring Harbor's public education programs, Nassau's museums and preserves, Hofstra and Adelphi public events, LIRR accessible cultural sites.

**Westchester and Hudson Valley:** the river towns' arts centers, the science barge and environmental programs, Westchester's nature centers, SUNY and private campuses' public calendars, the famous art center in a converted factory upriver (a legitimate day trip recommendation).

**New Jersey:** the science center on the waterfront, the performing arts center in Newark, Princeton's public lectures and youth programs, Montclair and Hoboken arts scenes, corporate HQ youth programs in the Jersey City and Newark orbit.

**Connecticut:** the maritime aquarium and science centers in lower Fairfield, Yale's public museums and lecture calendar in New Haven at the edge of the ring, Stamford corporate program announcements.

## 5.3 Ring 3 standing sources

Only checked on the deadline sweep rotation (Part 7), not every cycle: the selective residential summer programs at Boston, Providence, New Haven, Philadelphia, Princeton, and DC area universities; the national competitions headquartered in the corridor; the handful of museums and institutions with nationally unique teen offerings.

## 5.4 Ring 4 standing sources

Checked monthly: national virtual research mentorship programs with selective admission, major online competition platforms, virtual lecture series from national institutions, MOOC platforms only when a verified certificate connects to a student's documented thread.

## 5.5 Aggregators and discovery layers

Event aggregation platforms, city culture calendars, education news outlets that cover youth opportunities, school counselor newsletters that publish openly, and the opportunity lists maintained by other organizations. Aggregators are leads, not sources: everything found through an aggregator gets verified at the primary source before entering the feed, by protocol rule, because aggregators are where stale listings live.

# Part 6: The Ranking System

Every opportunity that enters the feed gets scored. The score sorts the review queue, drives the "top five this cycle" digest, and gives counselors a fast read on what deserves attention first. The system uses seven weighted dimensions summing to 100, plus modifiers.

## 6.1 The seven dimensions

**1. Timeliness (weight 20).**
How actionable is this right now? An application window closing in two to four weeks scores maximum (urgent but doable). An event happening within the cycle's window scores high. Something six months out scores low unless it requires long preparation (portfolio programs, hospital volunteering with clearance lead times, which earn a lead time bonus). Something whose date cannot be confirmed scores zero and goes to the verification queue instead of the feed.

* 18 to 20: deadline or date within 30 days, confirmed.
* 12 to 17: within 60 days, confirmed.
* 6 to 11: within 90 days, or further out but with justified long lead time.
* 1 to 5: further out, low urgency.
* 0: unconfirmed date. Does not publish.

**2. Accessibility (weight 18).**
Cost, geography, and eligibility combined. Free, Ring 1, transit accessible, no application scores maximum. Each barrier (cost, distance, selectivity of entry, eligibility restriction, age minimum) subtracts. Note that selectivity subtracts here but adds in dimension 5; this is intentional, because the two dimensions capture the real tension between reach and value, and the rubric should feel that tension rather than hide it.

**3. Stories value (weight 15).**
Does this generate narrative material? Concrete settings, sensory experience, human interaction, the possibility of surprise, and connection to a student's existing thread all score high. A virtual webinar scores low. A behind the scenes tour of a working institution scores high. The test question: could a student write 650 honest words that begin at this opportunity? 

**4. Scores value (weight 15).**
Does this generate credential, output, or measurable skill? Selective admission, evaluated work, certificates with real standards, recommender relationships, publishable or showable artifacts all score high. Attendance alone scores low.

**5. Selectivity and prestige (weight 12).**
Is being chosen for this meaningful, or is the institution's name itself a signal? A selective program at a named institution scores maximum. Open access at a named institution scores midrange. Open access at an unknown organization scores low on this dimension (but may still rank well overall through accessibility and Stories value, which is exactly how a hidden gem should score: high total, low prestige, with the blog doing the prestige work on its behalf).

**6. Blog and content potential (weight 12).**
Independent of student value: does this make content? Visual appeal, novelty, a counterintuitive angle (free beats paid, the museum program nobody knows, the community board meeting as college essay), seasonal hooks, and forwardability all score high. Some excellent student opportunities are content duds (important but dry deadline announcements) and some modest opportunities are content gold (a quirky one day festival). The feed serves both masters, so both get measured.

**7. Ladder connectivity (weight 8).**
Does this opportunity connect to other rungs? An exposure event at an institution with a teen council (attend now, apply later, with the application strengthened by attendance) scores maximum. A standalone event with no pathway scores low. This dimension operationalizes the ladder logic of 2.3 and rewards the compounding opportunities that build multi year arcs.

## 6.2 Modifiers

Applied after the base score:

* **Outer borough bonus (+5):** Ring 1 items in Queens, the Bronx, or Staten Island, to counteract the Manhattan default and serve borough equity.
* **Free flagship bonus (+5):** free items at named institutions, the heart of the value proposition.
* **Vetting risk penalty (minus 15 and a mandatory flag):** pay to play indicators per 2.4: high cost with open admission, credential language without evaluation, aggressive marketing to parents. These can still appear in the feed when families are likely to ask about them, but always flagged, never recommended unmodified.
* **Eligibility restriction flag (no point change, mandatory label):** residency, citizenship, school enrollment, age, or demographic eligibility requirements get stated verbatim from the source.
* **Stale risk flag (no point change):** found via aggregator or with a page not updated this cycle year. Must be primary source verified before publishing.

## 6.3 The tiers

* **85 to 100, Lead items:** top of the digest, candidates for immediate student matching and standalone blog treatment.
* **70 to 84, Strong items:** full feed entries, roundup blog material.
* **55 to 69, Worth knowing:** brief feed entries, useful for specific archetype matches even if not broadly compelling.
* **Below 55:** logged in the archive for pattern tracking but not surfaced, unless a counselor query specifically matches.

## 6.4 What the score does not capture

The rubric cannot know that a specific student needs exactly this thing this week, that a parent just asked about this exact topic, or that Megan needs a civic themed post for next Tuesday. Counselor and editorial judgment overrides the sort order freely. The score is the default ordering of attention, nothing more. When overrides happen repeatedly in the same direction, that is a signal to adjust the weights, which is what the iteration log in Part 14 is for.

# Part 7: The Recurring Research Protocol

This is the operating rhythm. The principle: small, frequent, rotating sweeps beat occasional exhaustive ones, because timeliness is the product and exhaustion is the failure mode.

## 7.1 Cadence and the three day rule

A research cycle runs **at minimum every 3 days**. The three day interval is chosen deliberately:

* Short enough that nothing with a two week deadline can slip through unseen. The worst case discovery lag for any newly posted opportunity is three days, which preserves at least eleven days of family reaction time on a two week window.
* Long enough that each cycle finds genuinely new material rather than rediscovering Monday's results on Tuesday.
* Frequent enough to sustain a twice weekly internal digest and a weekly external blog rhythm without ever running dry.

Cycles are anchored to the real current date. Every prompt run begins by establishing today's date and computing the cycle window from it. The system never publishes anything dated in the past, and treats "upcoming" as strictly future relative to the run date.

## 7.2 The rotation

Each cycle has a primary focus and a standing baseline, so that over any nine day period the whole territory gets covered while every cycle still checks the universals.

**Every cycle, no exceptions (the baseline sweep):**
1. Deadline check: any application windows opening or closing in the next 21 days, all fields, Rings 1 and 2.
2. One library system check (rotate among the three systems).
3. One outer borough anchor check (rotate Queens, Bronx, Brooklyn, Staten Island).
4. TED format and major lecture listings for the next 14 days.

**The rotating focus (one per cycle, repeating on a six cycle wheel, which covers all fields every 18 days):**
* Cycle A: Arts and Film deep sweep.
* Cycle B: STEM and Tech deep sweep.
* Cycle C: Humanities and History deep sweep.
* Cycle D: Civic and Social Impact deep sweep.
* Cycle E: Business and Entrepreneurship deep sweep (with anti-pattern filter at maximum).
* Cycle F: Health and Science of Care deep sweep.

**Monthly supplements (attached to whichever cycle falls nearest):**
* First cycle of the month: Ring 3 sweep (Northeast selective programs and competitions).
* Mid month: Ring 4 sweep (online, selective only).
* Last cycle of the month: seasonal preview (next month's known calendar landmarks from Part 12, verified against current year dates).

## 7.3 Cycle output contract

Every cycle produces, in this order:

1. **The pulse:** two sentences on what is happening in the ecosystem this window, written for the team.
2. **The ranked list:** 8 to 15 opportunities, scored per Part 6, in the schema of Part 10. Quality over quota; a cycle that verifies 8 real items beats a cycle that pads to 15 with maybes.
3. **The deadline board:** a standing list of every known open application window with its close date, carried forward and updated each cycle, so nothing depends on memory.
4. **The content shortlist:** the 2 or 3 items with the highest blog potential scores, each with a one line angle for Megan.
5. **The verification queue:** items found but not yet confirmed at a primary source, carried to the next cycle, never published.

## 7.4 Deduplication and memory rules

* An opportunity appears as a full entry once. Subsequent cycles reference it on the deadline board only, unless something material changed (deadline extended, new session added).
* Recurring events (a monthly free night) get one full entry per quarter at most, with a recurring tag.
* The archive of past entries is the institutional memory: which sources produce, which categories run thin in which months, which items the team actually used. Reviewing the archive monthly feeds the iteration log.

## 7.5 Failure modes and their countermeasures

* **The Manhattan drift:** countered by the outer borough baseline rule and bonus.
* **The arts drift** (arts events are the easiest to find, so lazy cycles overfill with them): countered by the rotation wheel.
* **The stale aggregator trap:** countered by the primary source verification rule.
* **The hallucinated event** (the catastrophic failure: publishing something that does not exist): countered by the hard rule that no item publishes without a live primary source confirmation found during that cycle's searches, plus the verified flag in the schema, plus human review before anything reaches a family. An invented opportunity sent to a parent would damage trust beyond what any quantity of good research repairs. When in doubt, the item goes to the verification queue, and the feed says so.
* **The quota pad:** countered by the explicit 8 item floor being acceptable and honest thinness being preferred to padding.
* **The deadline amnesia:** countered by the carried forward deadline board.

# Part 8: The Master Recurring Prompt

This is the prompt that runs every cycle. It is written to be executed by an AI research agent with live web search. Curly brace variables get filled at runtime; the rotation values come from the wheel in 7.2. The prompt deliberately restates the critical rules from this playbook because the agent executing it may not have the playbook in context.

```
You are the opportunity research engine for Wagner Prep, a New York City
college admissions counseling firm. You run on a recurring cycle. This is
an internal research task; your output goes to counselors for review, not
directly to families.

TODAY'S DATE: {{TODAY, e.g. June 9, 2026}}. Anchor every judgment of
"upcoming," "soon," and "deadline" to this real date. Never report
anything whose date has already passed. Compute all windows forward from
today.

THIS CYCLE'S ROTATION:
* Focus field for the deep sweep: {{FOCUS_FIELD, from the six field wheel}}
* Library system to check: {{LIBRARY_SYSTEM}}
* Outer borough anchor to check: {{OUTER_BOROUGH_SOURCE}}
* Monthly supplement, if this cycle carries one: {{SUPPLEMENT or "none"}}

YOUR AUDIENCE MODEL: High school students (grades 9 through 12) in the
New York City area, served through their parents, across six fields:
arts and film, STEM and tech, humanities and history, civic and social
impact, business and entrepreneurship, health and science of care.
Geographic priority: the five boroughs first (about 60 percent of
attention, with deliberate inclusion of Queens, the Bronx, Brooklyn, and
Staten Island, not only Manhattan), then the rail commutable belt of
Long Island, Westchester, North Jersey, and Southwest Connecticut (about
25 percent), then Northeast corridor selective programs only when they
clear a high bar (about 10 percent), then online opportunities as lowest
priority and only when selective or output producing (about 5 percent).

WAGNER PREP'S LENS: Every opportunity feeds one of two ledgers. STORIES
means narrative and experiential value: exposure, community, lived
experience, essay material. SCORES means credential and quantitative
value: selective admission, evaluated output, measurable skill, awards,
recommender relationships. Tag each item Stories, Scores, or Both. Also
tag each item's ladder rung: 1 Exposure (free, drop in), 2 Engagement
(recurring, low commitment), 3 Production (the student makes something
or holds responsibility), 4 Credential (selective, externally
validated).

WHAT TO SEARCH THIS CYCLE:
1. Baseline deadline sweep: application windows for high school
   programs, internships, competitions, councils, and fellowships in the
   NYC area that OPEN or CLOSE within the next 21 days. All six fields.
2. Events sweep: museum events, teen nights, TED and TEDx events, public
   lectures, festivals, screenings, and workshops in the NYC area within
   the next 14 days that a high schooler could attend, prioritizing free.
3. Library sweep: teen programs and events at {{LIBRARY_SYSTEM}} in the
   next 14 days.
4. Outer borough sweep: current teen programming at
   {{OUTER_BOROUGH_SOURCE}}.
5. Deep sweep of {{FOCUS_FIELD}}: go beyond events into application

   all four ladder rungs in this field, Rings 1 and 2.
6. {{SUPPLEMENT INSTRUCTIONS if applicable}}

  it in the verification queue, not the feed.

  "Ongoing" is acceptable only for recurring programs and must say so.
* Record cost exactly, including "free with registration" and any hidden
  costs. Prefer free. Flag any pay to play pattern: high cost, open
  admission, credential language without real evaluation. Score those
  with the vetting risk penalty and say why.
* Quote eligibility restrictions (age, grade, NYC residency, citizenship,
  school enrollment) precisely from the source. Do not soften them.
* Quality over quantity: 8 to 15 items. Do not pad. If a category ran
  thin this window, say so in the pulse.
* No dashes of any kind in any prose you write: no em dashes, no en
  dashes, no hyphens used as dashes. Rewrite around them.

SCORE each item 0 to 100 using these weights: timeliness 20 (within 30
days confirmed scores highest; unconfirmed dates score zero and do not
publish), accessibility 18 (free, Ring 1, transit reachable, open entry

scores value 15 (credential, output, recommender, selectivity),
selectivity and prestige 12, blog and content potential 12, ladder

institution, minus 15 plus a flag for vetting risk.

OUTPUT, in this exact order:
1. PULSE: two sentences on this window's ecosystem for the internal team.
2. RANKED LIST: each item with name, organization, field, ring, rung,
```

## 8.1 Runtime notes for whoever executes the prompt

* Fill {{TODAY}} from the actual current date at run time. This is the single most important variable in the system. A cycle run with a stale date produces dead listings and is worse than no cycle.
* Carry the previous cycle's deadline board into the prompt context when possible, so the board accumulates instead of resetting.
* The prompt fits within a single agent run with web search. Expect 10 to 25 searches per cycle. If the executing tool caps searches, run the baseline sweep first and the deep sweep second, because the baseline carries the timeliness obligation.
* Human review before anything external: the feed is internal until a counselor or Megan promotes an item.

# Part 9: Variant Prompts

The master prompt is the workhorse. These variants handle specific situations. Each inherits the hard rules, the date anchoring, the scoring system, and the voice requirements of Part 8; only the search mission changes. When running a variant, prepend the same TODAY'S DATE block and append the same HARD RULES and OUTPUT blocks from the master.

## 9.1 The deadline sweep (run when the team needs a pure deadline view, or monthly as an audit)

```
MISSION OVERRIDE: This cycle is a deadlines only audit. Ignore events.
Search for every application window for NYC area high school programs,
internships, fellowships, competitions, teen councils, and selective
summer programs that closes within the next 45 days or opens within the
next 30, across all six fields and Rings 1 through 3. For each: program
name, operator, open date, close date, eligibility verbatim, cost,
selectivity signals (acceptance rate if published, application
components), and primary source URL. Sort strictly by close date.
Cross check each found deadline against the operator's own page; many
third party lists carry last year's dates. State the page's evidence
```


## 9.3 The single student match (run on demand against a caseload need)

## 9.4 The vetting check (run when a parent forwards a program and asks "is this worth it")

```
plus open admission plus credential language without evaluation equals
vetting risk. Then find 2 to 3 alternatives in the NYC area that deliver

family facing framing that is honest without being dismissive of the

```

```
MISSION OVERRIDE: Preview {{NEXT MONTH AND YEAR}}. Using the seasonal
```

# Part 10: The Output Schema

Uniform structure makes the feed scannable, archivable, and automatable. Every full feed item carries these fields:

```
name:            Exact program or event title as the source states it
org:             Operating institution
field:           One of the six fields (plus cross field note if needed)
ring:            1, 2, 3, or 4 per the geography model
rung:            1 Exposure / 2 Engagement / 3 Production / 4 Credential
ledger:          Stories / Scores / Both
when:            Exact date(s), or application window with open and
                 close dates. Recurring items say the pattern.
location:        Venue, neighborhood, borough (or Virtual). Nearest
                 transit when useful.
cost:            Exact. "Free", "Free with registration", "$40 materials
                 fee", "Paid program, $X, scholarships available"
eligibility:     Verbatim restrictions, or "Open to all high schoolers"
url:             Primary source link
score:           0 to 100 with tier label
score_rationale: One line: what drove the score up or down
student_fit:     One sentence: archetype and grade band this serves
blog_angle:      One sentence pitch in brand voice
flags:           verified / verify_needed / vetting_risk / eligibility_
                 restricted / recurring / stale_risk (any that apply)
first_seen:      Cycle date it entered the feed
```

The digest format (what gets pasted to the team) compresses each item to four lines: title line (name, org, score), logistics line (when, where, cost), fit line, angle line. The deadline board is a simple sorted table: close date, program, field, eligibility short code, link.

# Part 11: The Editorial Pipeline

The feed's second life is content. This part maps feed output to Wagner Prep's blog and social rhythm so research effort converts to positioning.

## 11.1 The content thesis

Wagner Prep's content position, restated for this purpose: the calm local expert. Not the panic merchant ("Ivy admit rates hit new low"), not the generic advice mill ("10 essay tips"). The firm that demonstrably knows the city, tells you about the free thing first, and connects today's small action to the long arc of an application. The feed is what makes that position factually true rather than aspirational, because we will actually know what is happening before other people do.

## 11.2 The recurring formats

**The Radar (weekly roundup, the workhorse).**
Five to eight items from the week's cycles, organized by "if your student is into X." Always at least one outer borough item, always majority free, always with real dates. The Radar is the forwardable asset described in 3.3, and consistency matters more than brilliance: the families who receive it three weeks running start expecting it, and expectation is the beginning of authority.

**The Deadline Watch (monthly, calendar shaped).**
The deadline board, translated for families: what opens and closes next month, what to start preparing now. This format does the most direct admissions work because it converts our timeliness advantage into family action. It is also the format most likely to get pinned, printed, and shared in parent group chats.

**The Hidden Gem (biweekly, the trust builder).**
One under the radar opportunity given full treatment via the 9.6 deep dive. The implicit message of every Hidden Gem post: we look harder than anyone else. Outer borough items and library system items shine here.

**Free Beats Paid (monthly or as found, the contrarian service piece).**
A head to head: a famous expensive program versus a free or cheap alternative that reads as well or better to admissions offices, with honest analysis of when the paid one IS worth it. This format converts the 3.3 cost value misconception into the strongest trust building content we can produce. It requires the vetting check variant and a confident, fair tone; it should critique patterns, not mock specific families' choices.

**The Ladder Story (monthly, the framework piece).**
A narrative walkthrough of the 2.3 ladder in one field: from the free Saturday event to the selective program, with this season's real rungs named and dated. This is the format that teaches the Scores and Stories framework by showing rather than telling, and over time these posts become the evergreen library that explains what Wagner Prep believes.

**The Season Ahead (monthly, from the seasonal preview cycle).**
Next month at a glance. Pairs with the Deadline Watch; lighter, more invitational, Explorer archetype friendly.

## 11.3 Voice rules for feed derived content

* Lead with the action and the date, not the philosophy. The framework is the skeleton, never the opening paragraph.
* One opportunity, one clearly stated next step, one deadline per item. Families act on singulars.
* Say "free" early and often when true. Say costs plainly when not.
* Never imply an opportunity guarantees admissions outcomes. The honest claim is always about evidence and experience, not outcomes.
* No dashes of any kind, per house style.
* Every post ends with forward motion: what to do, by when.

## 11.4 The review gate

Nothing moves from feed to publication without: primary source re-verification on the publication day (dates move), eligibility language checked verbatim, and a counselor sanity read ("would I send this to a family I like"). The gate exists because the blog inherits the feed's biggest risk: one published dead link or wrong date costs more credibility than ten good posts earn.

# Part 12: The Seasonal Calendar

The NYC opportunity ecosystem has a rigid annual rhythm. Knowing it lets each cycle anticipate rather than merely react, and it powers the seasonal preview variant. Specific dates below describe recurring patterns; every current year date must be verified in cycle, which is the whole point of the system.

**September.** The ecosystem reboots. Cultural institutions launch fall programming; university public lecture calendars resume; teen council and afterschool application windows open at many museums for fall cohorts; the citywide open house weekend (normally closed buildings, free) lands in fall and is an Explorer goldmine; school year competitions announce. Feed posture: heavy events coverage, catch the fall application windows fast.

**October.** Peak fall: festivals, open studios across boroughs, science and ideas events, history programming. Many fall cohort deadlines close early in the month. Feed posture: events rich, begin previewing the winter deadline season in content so families are warmed up before it arrives.

**November.** Events continue; the major national teen art and writing award's regional deadlines historically land late fall to early winter and must be verified early because portfolios take weeks; first selective summer program applications open. Feed posture: deadline board begins its winter expansion; holiday season programming appears.

**December.** The quiet loud month. Public events thin out toward the holidays, but the most consequential application season of the year is opening: selective summer research programs, institutes, and fellowships overwhelmingly open applications December through January for the following summer. Feed posture: events light, deadlines heavy; the Deadline Watch becomes the lead format; remind families that winter break is application drafting time.

**January.** The single highest stakes feed month. Summer program deadlines stack January through February; the city's youth employment program window historically opens in winter; spring semester programs and cohorts open; science fair season begins. Feed posture: maximum deadline vigilance, every cycle's baseline sweep matters most now; thin event weeks are acceptable and should not be padded.

**February.** Deadline season crescendos and closes. Many summer selective programs close in February; spring teen council applications open at cultural institutions; spring festival season announcements begin. Feed posture: closing window urgency in content ("last call" items), start surfacing spring events.

**March.** The ecosystem wakes. Spring festivals, film festival season opens across boroughs, spring lecture series peak, competition regional rounds, late summer program deadlines (the second tier window for families who missed February). Feed posture: rebalance toward events while tracking the stragglers on the deadline board.

**April.** Peak spring: festivals, science and earth focused events around the environmental observance, open studios, spring performances, volunteer season ramps for summer roles. Hospital volunteer applications for summer placement need to move now given clearance lead times. Feed posture: events heavy, long lead time items get their moment.

**May.** Spring closes; summer previews. Free outdoor programming season announced (parks, outdoor film, festivals); last calls for unfilled summer programs; finals season means feed items should respect student bandwidth (low commitment exposure beats new applications this month). Feed posture: build the summer map for families.

**June.** Summer launch. Outdoor free season begins in earnest: parks programming, outdoor film series, festivals, museum summer hours; summer programs begin (content opportunity: what to do alongside a program, or instead of one if applications were missed); rising seniors begin essay season, which makes Stories framing content timely. Feed posture: rich free events, plus the first "next year" items (fall councils, next cycle previews) for the planners.

# Part 13: Quality Control and Verification

## 13.1 The verification standard

An item is verified when, during the current cycle, a primary source page was retrieved that confirms: the opportunity exists, its dates apply to the current year or cycle, and the stated logistics (cost, location, eligibility) come from that page. A search snippet is not verification. An aggregator listing is not verification. A page with no year stated and no recent update signals is partial verification at best and earns a stale risk flag.

## 13.2 The pre publication checklist

Before any item leaves the internal feed for a family or the blog:

1. Re-open the primary source on the day of sending or publication.
2. Confirm the date or window has not moved or closed.
3. Confirm eligibility language verbatim and that the target student or audience clears it.
4. Confirm cost language including registration requirements.
5. Confirm the link resolves and points to the right page, not a homepage.
6. For anything involving a minor attending alone: confirm age policies and end times.
7. Gut check: would I send this to a family I like, today, as is.

## 13.3 Honesty rules

* Unknown stays unknown. "Date not yet posted for this year, last year it was X, we are watching it" is a perfectly good feed entry and a trust building sentence. A guessed date is not.
* Thin cycles report thin. "Quiet week in civic, deadline season is over" is information.
* Corrections are content. If a published date moves, the correction goes out fast and plainly. The firm that corrects itself quickly is more credible, not less.
